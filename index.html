  1. Gothic Art

    1150-1600

    Gothic art is one type of Medieval art that characterized the 12th century and lasted until the start of the 17th century. The movement started in France and grew out of the rule of the Roman Catholic Church. Because many people were illiterate at the time, Gothic art and other types of Medieval art used consistent symbols and typology to convey the stories and ideas of the church. Art was almost strictly commissioned for and by the church and took the form of frescoes, sculptures, illuminated manuscripts, stained glass, and altarpieces.

  4. Renaissance

    1400-1700

    The Renaissance, which means "rebirth," marked a new interest in classical Greak mythology and scholarship. The influence of humanism as a cultural movement is evident in the art that was created throughout the period as subject matter shifted from Biblical stories to the perfection of the human body and mind. The movement started in Italy but also flourished beyond its origin in Florence.

  5. 1485

    The Birth of Venus is thought to have been commissioned by the wealthy Florence family the Medici's. The image highlights a key characteristic of Renaissance art, mythological subject matter. Here, the goddess Venus emerges from the sea.

    Birth of Venus, Boticelli

  6. 1486

    Da Vinci's Virgin of the Rocks marks an interesting contrast to previous treatment of the same subject matter. Rather than depicting the Virgin Mary, Jesus and John the Baptist with flat shapes in a very divine way, Da Vinci gave them idealistic forms and placed them in a totally improable setting, demonstrating his attention to human form and nature.

    Virgin of the Rocks

    Da Vinci loved science just as much as he loved painting. Botanists say his nearly flawless depiction of the small plants at the forefront of the painting are indicative of his hours of study and research.

  7. 1504

    David is perhaps the most well-known work by sculptor and painter Michelangelo, aside from the frescoes in the Sistine Chapel. David was a symbol of the civil liberties espoused by the then-Republic of Florence. Although he was orginally comissioned for the top of the Duomo, David was installed a central plaza to face Rome.

    David, Michelangelo

    David is 17 feet tall and now resides in the Galleria d'Accademia in Florence, Italy.

  8. 1517

    The Mona Lisa is one of the most well-known pieces of art. It is thought depict Lisa Gherardini, the wife of a wealthy Florentine merchant. The painting demonstrates that art at the time was reserved for the elite.

    Mona Lisa, Leonardo Da Vinci

  9. Rococo

    1715-1774

    Rococo art followed on the end of Baroque art which favored ornate details, order, and symmmetry. Unlike Baroque, Rococo is characterized by light pastel colors, asymmetrical design and saccharine playfulness.

  10. 1717

    Art historians argue about whether this painting depicts the group coming or going to Cythera, known as the island of love since it was thought to be Aphrodite's birthplace. It also demonstrates the Rococo color palette and wistfulness.

    Pilgrimage to the Isle of Cythera, Watteau

  11. 1767

    Frogonard's The Swing exemplifies the whimsical, carefree colors and feel characteristic of the Rococo movement. The scene, what with its overgrown garden and flouncy dress, demontrates an obsession with excess and comfort.

    The Swing, Fragonard

  12. Realism

    1830-1870

    Where Rococo and art prior emphasized the ideal and the comfortable, Realism demonstrated a refusal to put a facade over the life of the majority. Realism in art casts a beautiful spotlight on the commonplace.

  13. 1857

    The Gleaners was poorly received by the French upper class because it shows peasants in a positive and featured light. Millet and others at the time represented a break from aristocratic subject matter to everyday life of common people.

    The Gleaners, Millet

  14. Impressionism

    1867-1886

    This French art movement marked a break from traditional methods and the start of modern art. At the time, the impressionists were criticized for not painting things as they looked. The impressionists were more concerned with depicting light, human perception and the passing of time than accuracy.

  15. 1872

    The movement Impressionism was named for this painting. Established art critics disliked its blurry nature, but Monet wanted to capture the movement and passing of time over the water.

    Impression of a Sunrise, Claude Monet

  16. 1879

    Degas is known for his painting and sculpture of ballerinas. He was fascinated with portraying the human body in motion.

    Stage Rehearsal, Degas

  17. Post-impressionism

    1880-1920

    Post-Impressionism is formally defined as art beginning with Manet and those that followed him until the turn of the century. Post-Impressionist art is characterized by more realistic subject matter and a bolder use of color and emotion.

  18. 1882

    Manet shows a move away from Impressionist subject matter toward the modern art that would characterize Post-Impressionist art. Like many Impressionist paintings, Manet also demonstrated looser brush strokes that enable the viewer to see the artist's interaction with the canvas.

    A Bar at Folies Bergere, Eduoard Manet

  19. 1889

    Rodin was a French sculptor who demonstrated great emotion through his work. Unlike Classical Greek sculpture, Rodin's looks jagged, in the same way that the Impressionists allowed the viewer to see their brushstrokes but better feel the scene, Rodin focused more on the story and emotion conveyed than the smoothness of the sculpture itself.

    Burghers of Calais, Auguste Rodin

  20. 1891

    Gaugin was a French artist who spent much of his professional life painting the people of Tahiti. His depictions of the island are filled with vibrant color.

    Tahitian Women on the Beach, Paul Gaugin

  21. 1904

    Cezanne's work demonstrates an anxiety and focus on representing everything he could observe about the scene in front of him. He usually painted outside in front of his subject, just as many of the Impressionists had.

    Chateau Noir, Paul Cezanne

  22. Abstract Expressionism

    1940-1968

    This movement puts emphasis on the process of creating the work almost more than the work itself. It began and flourished in post-WWII New York. The work produced in this period reflects a cultural shift toward apoliticism, nihilism and experience. The name encompasses a large number of artists who largely have their own unrelated styles. Unlike in prior periods of art, Abstract Expressionism was just as much about the artist himself as the viewer and anticipated audience.

  23. 1951

    Riopelle was a Canadian artist and painter whose abstract work mimicked the technique of Jackson Pollock. This painting demonstrates his colorful paint dripping approach.

    1951, Jean-Paul Riopelle

  24. 1953

    Rothko is known for his soft, geometric paintings. He felt that his work allowed the viewer determine meaning on their own.

    No. 61, Mark Rothko

  25. Pop art

    1958-1970

    Pop Art effectively broke the divide between high art and pop culture. The major figures of the movement worked throughout the 1960s and 70s and included Andy Warhol, Roy Lichtenstein, Claes Oldenburg, and James Rosenquist among others.
